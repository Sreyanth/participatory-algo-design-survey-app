How does it work?

If you have ever thought to yourself “if the weather is hot I will go to the beach, and if it is not I will not go”, then you have used a decision tree for making decisions. Intuitively, this algorithm is very similar to how we make everyday decisions by using “if-this-then-that” rules. As we consider more factors, the algorithm keeps asking more questions until it has considered all the factors to reach a final prediction.

Let's try an example

To illustrate how it works, let’s take the example of predicting the sale price of a house listing in Ames, Iowa.

We first gather a list of all the factors that we think should be associated with sale prices. This may include the location, age, size, and surrounding amenities of the house and so on. We collect this information for the house we are selling and all the houses that were sold in this city in the past.

The algorithm will then make up rules based on what factors distinguish a cheap house from an expensive house in the past. For example, if the house has an area larger than 2,500 square feet, it will be on the more expensive side. It will be on the cheaper side otherwise. Next, if a house is more than 2,500 square feet and has heating, it will be approximately $100,000. It will be about $85,000 without heating. The list can go on.

To predict a price for the house we are selling, the algorithm will simply walk through a tree-like flow chart. After it reaches the bottom of the chart, we will know what price the house should have.

Advantages

The biggest advantage of the algorithm is that it is intuitive and easy to explain. Like its name suggests, you can draw a decision tree showing exactly how a decision is made. It is also better at capturing complex relationships among the pricing factors, and so can perform better than linear regressions in certain cases when such relationships exist.

Disadvantages

Since the algorithm builds the decision rules based on the houses it has seen, it can perform worse for houses it has never seen.
