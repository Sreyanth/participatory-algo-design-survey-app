How does it work?

This algorithm makes predictions by using outcomes of other a student’s score by using the scores of other students who have similar characteristics.

Let's try an example

To illustrate how it works, let’s take the example of predicting the sale price of a house listing.

This algorithm then works similar to a real estate agent who assesses your house and suggests a listing price. You first tell the algorithm/agent all the features that you think should be associated with the sales price such as the location, age, size, the surrounding amenities of the house, and so on. The agent will identify comparable houses in similar neighborhoods that have similar age, area, and other features to the house you are selling. A reasonable prediction for the price of your house should then be the average of the prices of these similar houses.

For example, if your house is in a good school district and about 2,000 square feet. The algorithm will find houses that are in similarly good school district with about the same indoor area. The average price of these houses will be about $3 million, which will be the suggested sale price for your listing as well.

Advantages

The algorithm can easily explain its predictions by referring to outcomes of similar houses. It can also capture the more complicated relationships between the factors and outcomes than linear regression algorithms.

Disadvantages

The main drawback of the approach is if we have to predict the price for a house that is like no other, it will be very difficult to do so because there is no other “neighbors” that look similar to the house. As a result it would make subpar prediction for this outlier.
