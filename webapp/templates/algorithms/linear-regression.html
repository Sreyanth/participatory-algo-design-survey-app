How does it work?

Linear regression makes a prediction by weighing factors associated with the outcome, and summing the weighted factors. It is called “linear” because it tries to fit a straight line between the factors and the predicted outcomes.

Let's try an example

To illustrate how it works, let’s take the example of predicting the sale price of a house listing in Ames, Iowa.

We first gather a list of all the factors that we think should be associated with sale prices. This may include the location, age, size, and surrounding amenities of the house and so on. We collect this information for the house we are selling and all the houses that were sold in this city in the past.

We can then fit a “linear” or a straight line to identify any relationship between the factors and the sale prices using information on houses that were sold in the past.

For example, if we use only the size of the house, we can see in the graph that every additional 100 sqft increases the house price by about $1,000. This algorithm might then look like: 

Predicted house price = -50,000 + 1000 * sqft

The house we want to sell has an area of 1,000 sqft, so the predicted house price is 

-50,000 + 1,000 * 1,000 (the area of our house) = 95,000

Of course, as we consider more factors, the graph will be more complex and each factor will have different weight. For example, while a larger area is associated with an increase in price, the age of the house is associated with a decrease in price. Such an algorithm might look like this: 

Predicted house price = -40,000 + 1000 * sqft - 1,000 * age

The house we want to sell has an area of 1,000 sqft, and is 10 years old, so the predicted house price is 

-50,000 + 1,000 * 1,000 (the area of our house) - 1,000 * 10 = 94,000

However, regardless how many factors we consider, the idea of weighing and summing them together to produce a prediction is the same.

Advantages

The advantage of this algorithm is that it is simple to implement. It is easy to explain the predictions of the outcomes by using the weights associated with the factors.

Disadvantages

However, because it assumes the relationship between the factors and the outcome can only be represented by a straight line, it does not capture more complex relationships like other more complicated algorithms do, if we don’t take care to create hints to the model. For example, a large house is good, but if it is located in a cold place, it would require lots of heating that can be a burden and decrease the value of the house. The algorithm can also be easily influenced by outliers. So if a house for reasons unknown to us is priced at an extremely high or low price compared to similar houses, it will mislead our algorithm and reduce its performance.
