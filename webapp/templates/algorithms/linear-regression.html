How does it work?

Linear regression predicts by weighing factors associated with the outcome and summing the weighted factors. It is called "linear" because it tries to fit a straight line between the characteristics and the predictions.

Let's try an example

To illustrate how it works, let's take the example of predicting the sale price of a house listing.

We first gather all the factors that we think should be associated with the sale prices, such as the location, age, size, the surrounding amenities of the house, and whether the home has painted walls, and so on.

We can then fit a "linear" or a straight line to identify any relationship between the factors and the sale prices using the information on houses sold in the past.

For example, if we use only the size of the house, we can see in the graph that every additional 100 sqft increases the house price by about $100,000. This algorithm might then look like: 

Predicted house price = -50,000 + 100,000 * living area in 100 sqft

So, if the house we want to sell has an area of 1000 sq.ft, the house price prediction will be:
-50,000 + 100,000 * 10 (the area of our house in 100 sqft) = 950,000

Of course, as we consider more factors, the regression equation will be more complex, and each factor will have a different weight. For example, while a larger area is associated with a price increase, the age of the house is associated with a price decrease. Such an algorithm might look like this: 

Predicted house price = -40,000 + 100,000 * living area - 10,000 * age

So, if the house we want to sell has an area of 1,000 sqft, and is 10 years old, the predicted house price will be
-40,000 + 100,000 * 10 (the area of our house in 100sqft) - 10,000 * 10 = 860,000

Regardless of how many factors we consider, the idea of weighing and summing them together to produce a prediction is the same.

Advantages

The advantage of this algorithm is that it is simple to implement. It is easy to explain the predictions of the outcomes by using the weights associated with the factors.

Disadvantages

However, because it assumes a straight line can only represent the relationship between the factors and the outcome, it does not capture more complex relationships like other more complicated algorithms do if we donâ€™t take care to create hints to the model. For example, a large house is good, but if it is located in a cold place, it would require lots of heating that can be a burden and decrease its value. Outliers can easily influence the algorithm. So if a house for reasons unknown to us is priced at an extremely high or low price compared to similar houses, it will mislead our algorithm and reduce its performance.
